1 INTRODUCTION
Shaders provide a method for rendering realistic and captivating imagery. Shaders have continued to evolve, starting as
simple programs for rudimentary rendering to complex algorithms that can render and simulate physical phenomena.
This project seeks to explore the capabilities of shaders through an intricate shader rendering.

2 CURRENT PROGRESS
Our team successfully implemented a shader code to visualize the Mandelbrot set, a complex fractal structure. The
shader maps each pixel’s coordinates to a point in the complex plane and iteratively applies the Mandelbrot equation
to determine whether the point belongs to the fractal set. By setting a high iteration threshold and adjusting the
coloring based on the number of iterations before the point escapes, our shader eectively highlights the boundaries
and intricate details of the set. The color gradient, from deep blue for points far from the set to vibrant orange for those
closer, provides a clear visual distinction of the fractal’s complex behavior. Additionally, we introduced dynamic visual
elements by enabling rotation based on the elapsed time, enhancing the interactive aspect of our visualization.

3.1 Problems encountered and Lessons Learned
In implementing the Mandelbrot shader code, our team gleaned several valuable insights. First, this shader’s iterative
nature underscores the importance of setting appropriate thresholds for computing the set. We also learned about the
mix function which performs a linear interpolation which is integral to how we color the Mandelbrot set shader.
Additionally, implementing the normalization of pixel coordinates taught us about maintaining aspect ratio correct-
ness and visual accuracy across dierent screen resolutions. The incorporation of time-based dynamic transformations,
such as rotation, added a layer of complexity and taught us more about animating shaders in response to time.
We also learned that zooming into the Mandelbrot shader can be very intensive on our computers. After our shader
zoomed enough, our computers would get hot or fans would start to speed up. This is interesting and may be useful to
look at through a ray-tracing perspective in order to nd ways to make the shader more ecient.
