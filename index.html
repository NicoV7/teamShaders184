Manipulating the geometric property, surface materials, and lighting, our team are able to render several visually distinct shaders. Our testing shaders resemble the early era of shaders, which focused on rendering basic geometry with flat colors and simple lighting. Our team has built upon these shaders to include sophisticated algorithms that are capable of simulating realistic lighting via ray tracing and path tracing.


  1. ‌CURRENT PROGRESS

    Our team successfully implemented a shader code to visualize the Mandelbrot set, a complex fractal structure. The shader maps each pixel’s coordinates to a point in the complex plane and iteratively applies the Mandelbrot equation to determine whether the point belongs to the fractal set. By setting a high iteration threshold and adjusting the coloring based on the number of iterations before the point escapes, our shader effectively highlights the boundaries and intricate details of the set. The color gradient, from deep blue for points far from the set to vibrant orange for those closer, provides a clear visual distinction of the fractal’s complex behavior. Additionally, we introduced dynamic visual elements by enabling rotation based on the elapsed time, enhancing the interactive aspect of our visualization.

    1. ‌Problems encountered and Lessons Learned

      In implementing the Mandelbrot shader code, our team gleaned several valuable insights. First, this shader’s iterative nature underscores the importance of setting appropriate frame buffer boundaries and thresholds for computing the set. We also learned about the mix function which performs a linear interpolation.

      Additionally, implementing the normalization of pixel coordinates taught us about maintaining aspect ratio correct-

      ness and visual accuracy across different screen resolutions. The incorporation of time-based dynamic transformations, such as rotation, added a layer of complexity and taught us more about animating shaders in response to time.
